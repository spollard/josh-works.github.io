# Lead Belaying in the Ground Fall Zone (A guide)

When your climber is beginning their climb, they‚Äôre in ‚Äúdecking range‚Äù, or ‚Äúthe ground-fall zone‚Äù. Until they are clipped to a bolt high enough above the ground that they cannot hit the ground if they fall, they are in ‚Äúdecking range‚Äù. In the next few minutes, we‚Äôll discuss how to give a good lead belay to your climber when in the ground fall zone.

Most of this article will discuss climbing in the ground fall zone when climbing indoors, for several reasons:

    When climbing inside, the first bolts are often quite close to the ground. Outdoors, first bolts are often high enough that once you‚Äôre clipped, you‚Äôre out of the ground fall zone. (This means you will either need a stick clip, or you‚Äôll be climbing dangerously high unroped to get to it‚Ä¶)
    When climbing indoors, there are situations where it‚Äôs OK for the climber to fall and end up standing on the ground. Outside, this is pretty much never the case, so you as the climber/belayer have more flexibility and room to practice what it‚Äôs like to catch falls low to the ground indoors.
    Most of us climb most often inside. By speaking just to indoor lead climbers, this guide can apply quite broadly without constant caveats to address how a climber/belayer might address the same situation outdoors.

# Why your Belayer is Keeping you from Climbing Hard(er)

Since climbing regularly again, I had a chance to observe lots of belaying in the gym.

I can‚Äôt walk up to a stranger and say ‚ÄúExcuse me, sir, I noticed that your poor belaying is totally crippling your climber‚Äôs ability to try hard, and actively eliminating any hope either of you have of improvement in this sport.‚Äù

It makes me feel for the climbers, though, because it‚Äôs difficult to improve at climbing with a bad belayer.

There‚Äôs a number of issues surrounding poor belaying, but they all tie back to either trust or competence.

The two can operate independent of each other:

    you can not trust a competent belayer (why don‚Äôt you trust them?)
    you can trust an incompetent belayer (whoops)
    you can not trust an incompetent belayer (good idea)
    you can trust a competent belayer. (Nirvana!)

While you can easily spot signs of an incompetent belayer, it‚Äôs much harder to determine if they are competent. Then, even if you‚Äôve established their competence, it takes time to build trust in them.

Here‚Äôs why bad belaying keeps you from improving ‚Äì even if you don‚Äôt actively consider the skill of your belayer (in other words, you‚Äôre trusting an incompetant belayer), your subconscious knows if they are competent and trustworthy. And if you‚Äôve not actively considered their skill, you probably don‚Äôt trust them.

So, how do you know level of the trust/competence graph you‚Äôre on?

Here are some indicators of unskilled (and untrustworthy) belaying:

    Lets the rope hang between the climber‚Äôs legs when climber is lower than the third bolt.
    Short ropes climber and doesn‚Äôt know it.
    Spikes the climber and doesn‚Äôt know it.
    Keeps rope too tight or too loose.

Now ‚Äì all of these are easily fixed. So ‚Äì this is where trust comes in. If your belayer is making these mistakes, and is either unaware or unwilling to fix them, why would you trust them? None of these mistakes puts the climber in grave danger, but all can make climbing much less fun.

# How to climb your first 5.10 (and then 5.11!)

You‚Äôll learn how to climb your first 5.10a (or V2, or V4, or 5.11a, or 5.12a) in this post, and more importantly, I‚Äôll give you a framework for reliable, continuous, and measured improvement. This framework will serve you for the rest of your climbing career.

    You‚Äôre already improving. If you want to improve, you probably are improving, or even if you feel like you‚Äôre not, you‚Äôre close to making more improvement, with the right approaches. For this, I salute you!

This current climbing barrier will be a mini-experiment for ‚Äúhow do you systematically improve at climbing‚Äù, and as you improve, you‚Äôll also improve at improving. (Crazy, right?)

In the beginning‚Ä¶ of your climbing career

# The Lawnmower Method of Rope Management

For all the lead climbers out there, there‚Äôs something you all do an awful lot of every time you lead climb and lead belay.

You feed out a lot of slack and you take in a lot of slack.

Right now we‚Äôre going to walk through how to take in a lot of slack quickly and safely.

I once got to happen upon the aftermath of an accident in a climbing gym. A woman was trying to take in slack, but was out of the brake position when her partner fell.

The concern, when you‚Äôre taking in a large amount of slack is that your climber could fall at any moment. This could happen right after they say take, or if they try to clip and drop the rope. You want to quickly take out a lot of slack.
